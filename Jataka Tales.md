The Jataka Tales is a collection of stories from ancient Buddhist sources. Below are 4 of them summarized:

***“The Haughty Slave"*** is about a slave woman becomes proud and disrespectful after gaining her mistress’s trust and kindness. Earlier, she worked sincerely and behaved humbly, but comfort and freedom made her lazy and arrogant. She stopped obeying orders and began acting as if she were equal to her mistress. To teach her a lesson, the mistress treated her strictly again. Realizing her mistake, the slave understood that pride and misuse of freedom lead to trouble. The story teaches that humility, gratitude, and good behavior are important, and that people should not become arrogant when they receive kindness or power.

In ***“The Pigeon and the Crow,”*** a kind pigeon lived peacefully near a kitchen and survived on simple food. A greedy crow became jealous of the pigeon’s comfortable life and tried to imitate him. Ignoring the pigeon’s advice, the crow entered the kitchen to steal fish and meat. The cooks caught him in the act, beat him badly, plucked his feathers, and threw him out. Injured and ashamed, the crow realized that greed and dishonesty lead to suffering. The tale teaches the importance of contentment, honesty, and respecting one’s own nature instead of blindly copying others.

In “***The Foolish Friend,”*** a king had a pet monkey whom he trusted greatly. The monkey was loyal but lacked wisdom and common sense. One day, while the king was sleeping, a fly kept disturbing him. Wanting to protect his master, the monkey tried repeatedly to chase the fly away. Finally, in anger, he picked up a sword and struck at the fly sitting on the king’s chest. Although the fly escaped, the king was killed by the monkey’s careless action. The story teaches that a foolish friend can be more dangerous than a wise enemy, and wisdom is more valuable than blind loyalty.

***"The Stupid Monkeys"*** is about In a group of monkeys that lived happily under the care of the gardener in a royal garden. One day, the gardener had to leave for some work and asked the monkeys to water the young trees and plants. Wanting to help, the monkeys eagerly agreed. However, their leader was foolish. He told the monkeys to pull out each plant before watering it so they could check the length of its roots. According to him, plants with long roots needed more water, while those with short roots needed less. The monkeys obeyed and uprooted every sapling and plant in the garden. After examining the roots, they replanted them carelessly and poured water on them. When the gardener returned, he found the entire garden ruined. The story teaches that foolish and ignorant people, even with good intentions, can cause great damage when they act without wisdom or knowledge.
