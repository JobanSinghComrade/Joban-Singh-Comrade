“Preludes”* by T. S. Eliot is an imagist poem that presents a miserable picture of urban life. The poem is divided into four sections, each giving us a glimpse into the repetitive and mundane routines of a city. Eliot begins by describing a winter evening filled with images of dirt, smoke, and decay. The setting feels lifeless and mechanical(non-human), with people returning home after a long, tiring day, suggesting a world drained of life and meaning.

In the second section, Eliot shifts to the early morning, where the city awakens in a dull and uninspiring way. The streets are filled with foul smells and dull scenes of everyday labour, focusing heavily on the emptiness of daily routines. People are shown to be trapped in a cycle of work and habit, moving mechanically like machines without a spiritual purpose. This reflects on the idea of modern urban life as spiritually empty and disconnected.

The third section focuses more closely on an individual, possibly representing any ordinary person. Eliot describes personal and somewhat uncomfortable details of daily life, such as lying in bed and becoming aware of one’s surroundings. These moments display a sense of inner unease and anxiety, as the individual is caught between awareness and helplessness. The personal experience mirrors the larger decay of the city.

In the fourth section, Eliot expands his view of humanity as a whole. He reflects on the idea that all people share this same meaningless existence. The imagery becomes more symbolic, suggesting that human life is filled with broken thoughts, disconnected experiences, and a loss of spiritual direction. The poem hints at a longing for meaning, but offers no clear solution.

In conclusion, *“Preludes”* is a powerful exploration of modern city life and its effects on the human soul. T. S. Eliot portrays a world where routine replaces meaning and individuals feel isolated and lonely despite being surrounded by others. The poem captures the sense of alienation, repetitiveness, and inner emptiness that defines much of modern existence, making it a key example of imagist poetry.





***Additional:*** Throughout *“Preludes,”* T. S. Eliot uses vivid, often unpleasant imagery—such as busy (sawdust-trampled) streets, smoky air, and worn-out objects—to reflect the moral and spiritual decay of modern society. His style is fragmented and impressionistic, mirroring the disjointed nature of the world he describes. The poem avoids traditional narrative flow, instead presenting scattered moments that build a larger picture of urban despair.
