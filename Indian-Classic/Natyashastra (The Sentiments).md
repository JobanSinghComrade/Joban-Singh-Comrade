**Q:6 What are the three types of Bhavas or States that Natyashastra describes?**

**Ans.** The Natyashatra describes 3 Bhavas - Dominant States, Transitory States, and Temperamental States.

The Dominant States are love, mirth, sorrow, anger, energy, terror, disgust and astonishment. 

There are 33 Transitory States, some of which are discouragement, intoxication, depression, weakness, arrogance, despair, and anxiety. 

There are eight Temperamental States - Paralysis, Perspiration, Horripilation, Change of Voice, Trembling, Change of Colour, Weeping and Fainting.

**Q:1 Explain the differences between Samgrah, Karika and Nirukta.**

**Ans.** Samgrah (or Digest) is a condensed overview of essential dramatic principles, primarily represented through Sutras(rules) and Bhashyas(commentaries). It serves as a fundamental summary, outlining key elements like Rasa(sentiments), Bhava(emotional state), and Abhinay(acting and stagecraft) to guide performers and scholars. It effectively connects theoretical knowledge with practical application in classical Indian drama.

Karika (or Memorial Verses) is that which captures the core essence of complexities regarding theatre, dance and drama. Such verses are often found alongside Sutras and commentaries and serve as foundational definitions of concepts in performance. They are helpful in adding memory and interpretation. They are crucial for understanding the technicalities of performance on the stage.

Nirukta (or Etymology) is that which comes up in connection with various words. It is helped by dictionaries and the rules of grammatical interpretation. It includes the meaning of the text involved, as well as the sound modifying it. In simple words, when the meaning of a word is established from the viewpoint of consideration of its root-word expressing such meanings in brief, are called etymology or Nirukta.

**Q:2 What do you understand by sentiment or 'Rasa'? Explain the "Rasa Theory" in detail.**

**Ans.** Sentiments (or Rasa) refer to the indivisible aesthetic essence of an artistic work or performance that arouses a feeling or emotion in the reader or the audience. However, its literal meaning is "juice, taste or flavour”. It is a concrete/sublime emotional state that arises from an association between the narrative, the actress and other elements in a course of theatrical dance or musical performance.

There are eight major Sentiments : Erotic, Comic, Pathetic, Furious, Heroic, Terrible, Odious and Marvellous. These sentiments arise from different emotional States. Each of the sentiments is produced by an interplay between Determinants, Consequents and Transitory states.

The Erotic Sentiment (Shringara) proceeds from the Dominant State of love. It is to be represented on the stage by Consequents such as clever movements of eyes, eyebrows, sweet words, etc. 

The Comic Sentiment (Hasya) has its base in the Dominant State of laughter. It is to be represented on the stage by Consequents such as throbbing of the lips, the nose, the cheeks, opening the eyes wide, unusual dressing or action. The purpose is to make the reader laugh or smile.

The Pathetic Sentiment (Karuna) has its base in the Dominant State of sorrow. It is to be represented on the stage by Consequents such as shedding tears, lamentation, drooping lips, etc.

The Furious Sentiment (Raudra) has its basis in the Dominant State of anger and wrath, creating actions like beating, breaking, crushing, fighting, etc.

The Heroic Sentiment (Veera) arises from the Determinants such as presence of mind, discipline, courage, or aggressiveness. It is related to bravado or heroism. It is to be staged by showing firmness, heroism, pride, valour, etc.

The Terrible Sentiment (Bhayanaka) has the Dominant State of fear as its basis. This is created by Determinants like sight of ghosts, panic, anxiety, etc. It is to be represented on the stage by Consequents such as trembling of the hands and the feet, change of colour, loss of voice, etc. 

The Odious Sentiment (Bibhasta) has the Dominant state of disgust as its basis. It is to be represented on the stage by Consequents such as shaking movement of all the limbs, narrowing down of the mouth, vomiting, etc.

The Marvellous Sentiment (Adbhuta) arises from the Dominant State of astonishment caused by the sight of heavenly beings or events, attainment of desired objects, magical acts, etc. It is to be represented through Consequents, such as by uttering words of appraisal, surprise, tears of joy, etc.

**Q:3 Compare and contrast any two Sentiments or 'Rasa' of your choice, on basis of the emotions they evoke, the situations that can endanger them and the media through which they can be depicted.**

**Ans.** The Pathetic Sentiment and the Terrible Sentiment are quite different, even though they have some similarities. The Pathetic Sentiment arises from the Dominant State of sorrow, whilst the Terrible Sentiment rises from the State of fear. Sorrow and fear are both negative Emotional States. The Pathetic Sentiment arises, for example, from the separation or death of a beloved person or hearing something unpleasant. The Terrible Sentiment is created when we hear hideous noise, the sight of ghosts, or battle, etc.

The Pathetic Sentiment is represented by sharing tears, lamentation, drooping lips, loss of memory, change of colour, etc.

The Terrible Sentiment has to be represented on stage by trembling of the hands or the feet, a change of colour, or a loss of voice. Some means of Consequents for the representation of those two sentiments are common, such as change of colours, inactivity, fear, loss of voice, dryness of mouth, etc.

**Q:4 Do you think that a lucid comprehension of 'rasa' can enrich the experience of witnessing a performance through any artistic medium? Why/why not? Support your answer with examples.**

**Ans.** A lucid comprehension of “Rasa” can, indeed, enrich the experience of a theatrical performance by turning it from passive viewing to active participation. It transforms a spectator into “Rasika”, who can sense the emotional, psychological, and spiritual depth of the artwork. The understanding of the Erotic Sentiment enables one to feel the emotional state of the 2 lovers, Portia and Bassanio, in Shakespeare’s play The Merchant of Venice and of Miranda and Ferdinand in Shakespeare’s play The Tempest, in a better way. We are so moved by the emotional state of the lovers that we share their emotional world.

A grasp of Sentiment, represented on the stage, helps the viewer to interpret specific gestures and movements of the actors better. For example, if one knows about the Pathetic Sentiment, they can have a better understanding of the sad gestures of Devdas, enacted by Dilip Kumar in the old film “Devdas”. Thus, lucid comprehension of a Sentiment enables the audience to understand that the performer is not simply sad, creating a carefully crafted sentiment and enabling a deeper experience of artistry. And enables the viewers to experience feelings like sorrow, despair, or fear in a safe place, leading to purification of emotion; much similar to Aristotle’s Catharsis.

**Q:5 How are the different Comic Sentiments expressed?**

**Ans.** The Comic Sentiment gets expressed in light smile, gentle laughter, laughter of ridicule, vulgar laughter, and excessive laughter. The Slight Smile of the people of the superior type should be characterized by slightly blown cheeks and elegant glances, and teeth are not to be visible. Their Smile should be distinguished by blooming eyes, face and cheeks, and it should have slightly visible teeth. The Gentle Laughter should have a slight sound, sweetness, and should be suitable to the occasion. In it, eyes and cheeks should be contracted, and the face should be joyful. During the Laughter of Ridicule, the nose should be expanded, the eyes should be squinting, and the shoulder and the head should be bent. The laughter on occasions not suitable to it, the laughter with tears in one’s eyes, with the shoulder and the head violently shaking, is called Vulgar Laughter. The Excessive Laughter is that in which the eyes are expanded, and a cheerful sound is loud and excessive, and the sides are covered by hands.
