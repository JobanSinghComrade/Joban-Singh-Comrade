In the beginning, there was neither existence nor non-existence—no sky, no space, and no clear distinction of anything. The hymn raises a profound question about what could have triggered creation in such a void. There was neither death nor immortality, and no division between night and day. Only “the One” existed, breathing without air, sustained entirely by its own inner power. From this mysterious, self-sufficient presence, everything came into being, including its own origin.

The ancient seers, or Rishi Munis, reflected deeply on this mystery. They questioned how existence could arise from non-existence and wondered whether the source of creation lay above or below. Despite their deep examinations, they could only sense that some unknown and powerful force governed everything. Their inquiry highlights both human curiosity and the limits of understanding when faced with the origins of the universe. Ultimately, the hymn does not provide definite answers but instead embraces uncertainty. It asks who can truly know how creation happened or from where it emerged. In a striking conclusion, it suggests that perhaps creation formed itself, or perhaps it did not. The possibility remains that even the highest power may not fully know the truth, leaving the origin of existence an eternal mystery.

The Burial Hymn of the Rig Veda reflects early Vedic ideas about death, the soul, and the afterlife. It is mainly concerned with guiding the departed soul peacefully to the next world. The hymn invokes Yama, regarded as the first mortal who discovered the path to the world of the dead. He becomes the ruler of the ancestors, and the departed soul is encouraged to follow his path and join the forefathers in a peaceful realm. The dead person is asked to leave behind all earthly suffering and move toward the afterlife. The hymn expresses hope that the soul will find happiness, light, and reunion with ancestors. There is no strong fear of death; instead, death is seen as a transition rather than an end.

The physical body is returned to nature. The hymn speaks of burial (and sometimes, cremation in later traditions) as a way of restoring the body to the earth, while the soul continues its journey. Natural elements like earth, fire, and air are invoked to protect and gently receive the body. At the same time, the hymn offers comfort to the living. It encourages them to return to life, continue their duties, and not be consumed by grief. Life and death are presented as parts of a natural cycle, governed by universal order.

Overall, the Burial Hymn presents a calm, philosophical acceptance of death, emphasizing continuity, reunion with ancestors, and the eternal journey of the soul.
