Hitopadesha is collection of classical texts carrying moral tales and fables written by Narayna Pandit.

“The Foolish Tortoise” from the Hitopadesha tells the story of a talkative tortoise whose friends, two swans, try to save him from danger by carrying him through the air with a stick. They warn him not to speak, as he must hold the stick tightly with his mouth. During the journey, people below mock the strange sight, and the tortoise, unable to control his urge to respond, opens his mouth to speak. As a result, he falls to the ground and dies. The story teaches the moral that lack of self-control, especially over speech, can lead to one’s downfall.

“The Quick-Witted Wife” - In Vikramapura, there lived a merchant named Samudra Datta whose wife, Ratnaprabha, secretly had a relationship with one of his servants. One day, Samudra Datta caught Ratnaprabha kissing the servant. However, Ratnaprabha was very clever and quickly created an excuse to save herself. She told her husband that the servant had been stealing and eating camphor from the house and that she was only smelling his mouth to check whether he was guilty.

Her husband believed her explanation. The servant, however, became shocked by the woman’s cleverness and deceit. He thought it was dangerous to stay in a house where the mistress could twist any situation in her favor. Feeling uncomfortable, he decided to leave, though the merchant persuaded him to remain.

The story highlights the importance of quick thinking and presence of mind in difficult situations. At the same time, it also shows how intelligence can sometimes be used for deception and manipulation.

“The Short-Sighted Crane” from Hitopadesha tells of a foolish crane who befriends a cunning crab. Pretending concern for drying waters, the crane offers to carry fish to a safer lake but secretly eats them. When it tries the same trick on the crab, the crab grows suspicious. Midway, it discovers the crane’s deceit and kills it by cutting its neck with its claws. The story highlights the danger of blind trust and the importance of intelligence and caution. It teaches that greed and deceit ultimately lead to one’s downfall, while awareness and wit ensure survival.

In "The Hermit and the Mouse", a kind hermit rescues a small mouse from a predator and brings it to his hut for safety. He cares for it, feeding and protecting it. Over time, the mouse grows stronger and, through the hermit’s spiritual power, is transformed into a beautiful maiden. A cat threatens her, so she is changed into a cat; later a dog, then a tiger, each time to escape danger. Eventually she becomes arrogant and forgetful of her humble origin. The hermit realizes pride has overtaken her and restores balance, teaching humility. The story highlights gratitude, dependence on guidance, and the dangers of pride, showing that blessings without humility can lead to downfall and confusion in life and growth indeed.
