## Short Answer Question

**Q:1 Why did the land owner wish to bury his money in the ground?**

**Ans:** The land owner was an old man. He had a young wife who had borne him a son and a heir. One day, he thought that after his death his wife might marry again as she was young, and spend all his money instead of handing it over to his son. Because of this suspicion in his mind, he buried his money in the ground. It was ironic that he suspected his own wife but not his slave whom he trusted completely. 

---

**Q:2 What change did Nanda’s behaviour undergo in the forest? What was the reason behind it?**

**Ans:** The slave Nanda knew where the land owner had buried his money in the forest. He behaved normally when he would go with the land owner’s son to the place of the treasure, but the moment he reached the place he would become abusive. This happened twice. He would lose his temper and address his master's son in a very rude manner, “You servant of a slave-wench’s son! How should you have any money here?” The reason behind the change in his behaviour was his evil intention to steal his master's money. It was greed that caused him to speak impudently to his master's son.

**Q:3 What proposal did the crow put forth? What was the pigeon’s reply to it? What did he warn the crow against? **

**Ans:** In the story ‘The Pigeon and the Crow’, the crow put forth the proposal of a companying the pigeon (Bodhisatta) Wherever he went because he had won his admiration. The pigeon’s reply was that it would be difficult for the crow as his(pigeon’s) kind of food and the crows we're not the same. The crow insisted on following him, saying that he would feed on his kind of food by his side. The pigeon warned him against dishonesty, suspecting his intentions.

**Q:4 What strategy did the monkeys employ to water the plants the best way? **

**Ans:** The monkeys thought that they must not waste water, and water the plants according to their needs. They used the strategy to pull out each plant to see if its roots were deep or not. They gave plenty of water to those whose roots struck deep, but only a little water to those with the tiny roots. Thus, in their foolishness they uprooted and harmed all the plants. 

---

**Q:5 Whom would you blame for killing the trees- the monkeys or the gardener? Why? **

**Ans:** We can blame the gardener for killing the trees. First of all, he should never have thought of going to the festival leaving his duty of watering the plants to monkeys. Secondly, if he had to go, he should have first water the plants or given his duty to some responsible person. Monkeys were not so blameworthy as they seem to be. Monkeys are mischievous by nature. Moreover, they are not wise enough to know that uprooting the plants means killing them. 

---

**Q:6 Why were the robbers christened “Dispatchers”? **

**Ans:** In the story ‘The Robbers and the Treasure’ we are told that the robbers were called Dispatchers. Of every two Prisoners they made, they would dispatch one to fetch the ransom, and that is why they were called the dispatchers. The other person they kept as surety. If they captured a father and a son, they would send the father for ransom to free his son. When they caught the Vedabbha-brahmin and his disciple, the Bodhisatta, they sent the Bodhisatta for the ransom and kept the brahmin with them. 

---

**Q:7 What caution did the Bodhisatta ask the Vedabbha-brahmin to exercise? How did the latter suffer for not paying heed to it? **

**Ans:** Before going to fetch the ransom, the Bodhisatta told the brahmin that there would be the conjunction of the planets that day, which brought about the reign of the Things of Price. He cautioned him not to repeat the charm and bring down the precious shower, or he might bring calamity for himself and the band of robbers. The brahmin got killed by a robber of another band, and all the robbers got killed in violent fights among themselves, because he had brought down thoughtlessly the precious treasure from the heavens by his charm. 

---

**Q:8 What did the carpenter’s son intend to do? What did he end up doing, instead? **

**Ans:** The carpenter’s son wanted to drive away the mosquito on the head of his father. He ended up cutting his fathers head in twain, with the stroke of a sharp axe on his head. His father died on the spot. It shows that a foolish person despite his best intention ends up causing some harm.

**Q:2 Explain “sense-lacking friends are worse than foes with sense.”Do you agree?Why or why not? **

Ans:- There is no doubt that sense- lacking friends are worse than foes. It is so because our attitude towards our friends is complacent. We can never think that they can harm us in any way, whether they are wise or stupid. On the other hand, we remain alert toward our enemies as we know that they can harm us anytime. Foolish friends who intend to do good end up in harming us, because we are complacent, while enemies often fail to harm us because we do not let them come near us ordinarily to hurt us in any way. This is what we see in the story 'The Foolish Friend’. In the story, the Carpenter with bald head, asks his son to drive away mosquito sitting on his scalp. The son, in order to obey his father’s command, to comfort him, strikes his father’s head with a sharp axe in his hand, to drive away the mosquito. In doing so, he cuts his father’s head into two, thus killing him on the spot. This is what we find in the story ‘The Stupid Monkeys’. The monkeys ,living in the royal garden were friendly with the gardener. In order to fulfil the gardener’s wish that they should water the plants in his absence, they started pulling up all the plants one by one. They wanted to save the water by giving plenty of water to those whose roots struck deep and only a little to those with the tiny roots.. By doing so, the stupid friends of the gardener killed all the plants.

**Q:3 What havoc did greed work on various characters in “The Robbers and the Treasure”? **

Ans: In the story ‘The Robbers and the Treasure’ greed is the cause of a large number of killings. By ignoring the wise advice of Bodhisatta the Vedabbha- Brahmin brought down the heavenly treasure so that by giving it to his captors as ransom he would get free. He did not wait for his disciple, the Bodhisatta ,who had gone to fetch the ransom. The robbers freed him, collected their booty and went away. The brahmin followed them. Unluckily ,all of them were captured by another band of 500 robbers. The first band of robbers suggested the other to ask the brahmin to get for them the treasure the way he had got for them. When the brahmin were asked to repeat the charm and produce the treasure, he said that he could do so only after a year when there would be a specific conjunction of planets. The second band of robbers got angry and one of them cut him into two with a sharp sword. Then the second band of robbers chased the first band of robbers, they killed every man of them in hand- to- hand fight. Then the robbers Fought among themselves for the treasure they had snatched from the first band of robbers. Each one of them wanted to possess the booty without sharing it with others. The result was that only two were left alive. When the two survivors managed to carry off the treasure ,they hid it in the forest near a village. Feeling hungry, one of them went to the village for food, the other sat there, with a sword in hand, to guard the treasure. When the robber returned with food, the other robber cut him into two with his sword. He did not want to share the treasure with him. Then he ate the food. He died then and there, as the food had been poisoned by the first robber as he too was unwilling to share the treasure with his companion. Thus, the brahmin and all the robbers perished because of greed.

**Q:4 Discuss the use of symbolism in the prescribed stories? **

Ans: The Jataka Tales use primary symbols to represent or convey the Buddhistic virtues and vices. In the story ‘The Robbers and the Treasure’’, the heavenly treasure symbolises worldly pleasures which when pursued blindly lead to suffering, discord and death. It underlines the fleeting and dangerous nature of material gain which symbolises a trap in Buddhism. The robbers who get into this trap are killed one by one. The two group of robbers symbolise ignorance and hatred which are a part of the three evils in Buddhism. Their mutual slaughter demonstrates that greed is self- destructive. The Vedabbha -brahmin represents foolish wisdom or misplaced knowledge. He lacks the wisdom to know when to use it safely. He is foolish to create the treasure in the presence of those who, will kill him on any pretext . The poisoned food symbolises deceit, treachery , the fatal culmination of greed. The forest in the story represents a place where the ego is free to operate without moral restraint. The moral of the story is also symbolic-that the pursuit of material wealth at the cost of virtue and life leads to both physical and spiritual death. ‘The Pigeon and the Crow’ uses symbolic characters and setting to Illustrate the consequences of greed and the virtue of contentment. The pigeon is described as gentle and satisfied with eating simple food like grass seeds. He symbolises a life free from greed. On the other hand, the crow represents uncontrolled desire, insatiable hunger and foolishness. He also represents those who ignore good advice from wise friends leading to suffering and even death. The kitchen in the story forms a dangerous setting. With its rich smells of fish and meat, it represents a trap of worldly pleasures and temptations. The crow gets into its trap easily because of his greedy nature. The cook who tortures the crow for his misdoing represents the law of karma. Those who do evil acts have to suffer in the end. The law of karma is inevitable. The story provides a symbolic lesson that greed or uncontrolled desire for worldly pleasure is the source of suffering and destruction. In the story ‘The Haughty Slave’, the slave represents pride or haughtiness. In his greed to steal his master’s hidden treasure he loses self-control and becomes abusive. On the other hand ,the son of the landowner represents self-control and humility that ultimately wins over pride. In the story ‘The Foolish Friend’, the carpenter ‘s son represents foolishness. Despite his best intention he does harm to his own father. This is what we see in the story ‘The Stupid Monkeys’. Monkeys represent foolishness. In the absence of knowledge they do more harm than good.

## Theme

**Q: "Jataka Tales not only serve as engaging folklore, but also effective teaching tools to drive complex ethical principles home, using simple language." Discuss the preceding the statement in the light of the stories The Haughty Slave, The Pigeon and the Crow, The Foolish Friend, and The Robbers and the Treasure.**

**Ans:** The Jataka Tales are a subset of Tripitaka (a collection of Buddhist stories) that uses the figure of Bodhisatta(Gautam Buddha) in various incarnations throughout these stories to drive each story towards a certain understanding of a different set of morals. They are not just entertaining stories, but also powerful tools for teaching moral values in a simple and clear way. Each story teaches deep lessons on morality and ethics through everyday situations and relatable characters. In The Haughty Slave, the story teaches that pride and arrogance can lead to one’s downfall. The slave’s behaviour shows how disrespect and ego harm relationships. In The Pigeon and the Crow, the contrast between the wise pigeon and the greedy crow highlights the importance of good company and self-control. The crow’s foolish actions ultimately bring trouble, teaching that bad habits and wrong choices have consequences. Similarly, The Foolish Friend shows that a foolish companion can be more dangerous than an enemy. The friend’s lack of wisdom causes harm despite good intentions, stressing the value of intelligence and careful thinking. In The Robbers and the Treasure, greed and dishonesty lead to destruction, as the robbers turn against each other instead of sharing with one another. Through such simple storylines and characters, these stories explain the importance of complex ideas like wisdom, being humble, and the consequences of human actions. Thus, Jataka Tales effectively combine entertainment with moral education, making ethical lessons easy to understand and remember.
